# LMAP — Device Compliance & Access Control

*Specification / design note. Drafted 2026-07-04. **Status: DRAFT for review.** Once settled, this drives updates to `PROTOCOL_LAYERS.md` §4.2–4.5, `PROTOCOL_POSITIONING.md` §7–8, and the whitepaper §7–9 (see §8). Working sections (§8–9) are editorial and are removed before publication.*

---

## 1. Principles

Under LMAP, playback of protected content occurs on a **compliant device** — a player meeting a published hardware conformance specification (§3) — and decryption is gated by **current on-chain ownership** of the entitling token. Access control is **native to the protocol**: it depends on no external key-management service. (At bootstrap a single Foundation-operated issuer performs key issuance under this rule — a temporary centralization internal to the protocol's own standards body, not a rented external dependency; §5 dissolves it into a threshold network with no single trusted party.)

This independence is a protocol requirement, not a preference. A participant's access to content they own must never be contingent on a third party's continued operation or business decisions; an access layer rented from an outside service would make every holder's ownership revocable by that service's fate — the dependency LMAP exists to remove. (Downloaded content therefore plays indefinitely offline; see §6.)

**Compliant hardware is required; a particular manufacturer's hardware is not.** The requirement is a **public conformance specification** any manufacturer can build to — making concrete the protocol's existing commitment that "any conformant device can participate" (whitepaper §9). Certification (§4) verifies a device model against the public criteria; it is open, non-discriminatory, and privileges no implementer. Reference implementations exist to be copied, not to gate.

**Two properties are kept conceptually distinct** — conflating them is a known category error, since they solve different threat models:

| | Access control | Endpoint protection |
|---|---|---|
| Question | *Who may obtain the key?* | *What may the device do with decrypted frames?* |
| Answer | Current on-chain ownership (`balanceOf > 0`) | A compliant device (secure element, in-perimeter decrypt) |
| Nature | **Ownership-cryptographic** — the protocol ideal, hardware-independent in principle | Hardware — supplied by compliant devices |
| Enforced by | The ownership check at binding (wallet signature + `boundCount ≤ balance`) and the blinded-commitment release rule (§2) — decentralizing to a threshold network (§5). Transfer itself is never gated. | Device attestation (§3, §4) |

Access control is never "you must own particular hardware." It is "you must own the token." Compliance is what makes it *safe to hand the key to an endpoint* — an endpoint-protection concern, not an access-control one.

### 1.1 Aligned incentives — why openness serves every participant

Networks thrive when incentives are aligned among their participants. LMAP's openness — any manufacturer may build a compliant device, any operator may run a storefront — is not a concession; it is the mechanism by which the network compounds in value for everyone on it. The logic, stated plainly so it is visible:

- **Every compliant device is capacity for the whole network.** A device that stores and seeds content adds replicas, upload bandwidth, and redundancy for *all* titles — supplied by the device owner. The network behaves as a **grassroots, member-owned CDN** whose performance and resilience *rise* with total device count, across every manufacturer. Delivery cost per stream *falls* as the fleet grows; no one carries the centralized curve where success is expensive.
- **A multi-vendor fleet is more secure than a monoculture.** The native threshold layer (§5) grows stronger and more available as independent nodes join, and manufacturer diversity removes single-vendor compromise and single-point-of-failure risk. More participants harden the protection that protects them all.
- **More participants mean more liquidity, and liquidity is the value.** Owned media is worth having in proportion to where it can be bought, lent, resold, and played. Additional storefronts and players deepen that liquidity, raising the value of every title for every holder. Participants grow the market together; they do not divide a fixed one (`PROTOCOL_POSITIONING.md` §4).
- **An open standard outlasts a closed one.** A standard anyone may implement accrues adoption a proprietary one cannot; openness is how the protocol endures rather than being displaced by something more open.
- **Openness differentiates rather than commoditizes.** Every LMAP title is available in every storefront, yet storefronts do not collapse into identical price-takers. The storefront that *mints* a title sells it at the natural price floor (its fee is set into the mint); any other storefront reselling it must add its own fee on top, so for them the title is about exposure and retention, not price leadership. Storefronts therefore compete on the two things openness *cannot* level: **selection** (the desirable originals they bring to mint) and **curation** (in a gateless registry anyone can mint anything, so which titles a storefront chooses to represent *is* its brand). Openness commoditizes the plumbing and frees competition to happen where it should — on taste and on what you make.

And the property that keeps this honest rather than naive: **the protocol grants no participant a *lock-in*.** No implementer can trap users or content behind a switching cost the protocol itself creates. The advantages that do exist — the minter's per-title price floor above, a first mover's brand, the best client experience — are *earned* and must be continuously re-earned; none is captive. That discipline is exactly what protects users: the incentive to excel never relaxes into captivity. Openness and quality reinforce each other instead of trading off.

## 2. Access-control model — binding (day 1)

A title's master key is random at content preparation and gated by **current** on-chain ownership of the entitling token (`balanceOf(wallet, tokenId) ≥ 1` for personal viewing; higher thresholds unlock exhibition tiers — see the per-tier condition in §2.1). The day-1 mechanism is **attested per-device binding**: a copy is bound to a compliant **direct player** (a sealed device with its own video output — §3) — the mechanism `PROTOCOL_LAYERS.md` §4.3 specifies, here elevated from a future tier to the launch mechanism.

**Binding is a count, not a flag.** The entitling token is a *fungible* ERC-1155 unit; there is no distinguishable "the token" to mark. Binding is therefore tracked as a **count per `(wallet, tokenId)`** — `boundCount` — under one invariant:

> **`boundCount ≤ balance`, and transferable units = `balance − boundCount`.**

A wallet may bind as many copies as it owns units (one per household player, say), and may transfer only its *released* units. Scarcity holds — a wallet can never have more live copies than it owns units — *without* any ongoing ownership check: enforcement happens only at the discrete moments of bind and release, never by surveilling playback. Playback stays sovereign; only transferability is gated.

**The enforcement locus is a separable layer (the future-proofing), and it never becomes a gate on exchange.** The token is, and remains, a **plain ERC-1155** — minted once, valid under every phase of this design. Binding state lives in an **on-chain, world-readable binding registry from launch** — a separate accounting contract — so a unit's released status is publicly verifiable without trusting a private ledger. **Its writers are the compliant players themselves, under a blinded-commitment rule** that lets a device release its *own* copy without a wallet co-signature and without ever exposing device identity. A **bind** is accepted only when it carries a valid, non-revoked compliant-device credential *and* the owner's wallet signature, with the registry enforcing `boundCount ≤ balance` against the token's `balanceOf`; the write records a **blinded binding commitment** — `C = hash(secret)` for a fresh secret the device keeps — *never* the device's identity. A **release** is authorized by *opening* that commitment (revealing the secret's preimage): opening proves the writer is the device that bound, so release needs no wallet signature and reveals no device identity. This is load-bearing twice over — it closes the authorization gap the self-audit would otherwise have (at self-audit the binding wallet has *sold* the token and is gone, so it cannot co-sign), *and* it keeps device identity off-chain so the registry never becomes a public device ledger (§13). Any certified device can *write*, but only the device that opened a given bind can release *that* unit; a certified attacker cannot falsely release someone else's units, because it cannot open a commitment it did not create. **No central issuer writes the flag** — the attested fleet does, from launch. The one wallet-authoritative write is **recovery**: after the report window the owner force-releases their *own* `(wallet, tokenId)` units — the registry is indexed by wallet, so the owner enumerates their own titles and a lost device that can no longer open its commitments is not required. **What a single party *can* do at bootstrap** is revoke a device's write capability (certification, the recognized-roots list, and revocation are Foundation-held at launch), which pushes that holder onto the 30-day recovery path; it *cannot* prevent the release, precisely because recovery is wallet-authoritative. The honest claim is therefore that a single party can *delay* a release, not *withhold* it (revocation is policy-limited to genuine compromise — §4 — but that is policy, not mechanism). The key issuer (§4) is confined to *key wrapping* — enabling decryption at first bind — which is separate from flag state and decentralizes on its own path (§5). What does **not** exist in any phase is a power to block a transfer: **the registry *informs*; it never *gates*.** The token transfers freely on-chain always — the protocol lets no party, issuer included, block the exchange of an owned token. (A contract-level transfer rule is conceivable only as an *opt-in*, per-title property a publisher could elect — never a protocol-wide veto; open question (§9), not a committed step.) **What the chain actually verifies (an implementation item, scoped honestly).** A registry write checks an **enrolled device-key signature** plus a **non-revocation proof** against the recognized-roots / revocation registry (§4) — *not* a fresh attestation report: evaluating firmware measurements against an approved-manifest list on-chain is impractical, so the chain guarantees *certification-at-enrollment and non-revocation*, not integrity-at-write-time (fresh firmware integrity is proven to the key issuer at wrap time, off-chain — the two checks are complementary). The reference secure elements sign **P-256**, which the EVM's native `ecrecover` cannot verify; Polygon's **RIP-7212 P-256 precompile** makes this verification cheap and is the assumed primitive. **Who submits and pays:** a self-audit release fires with no owner present, so the *device* submits that transaction — needing either its own funded submission key (distinct from the P-256 attestation key) or a relayer/paymaster; provisioning and gas for device-submitted writes are an open implementation surface. **Ordering:** the key issuer wraps *only on a confirmed registry write*, so a wrap can never land against a bind that failed to record (no keyed-but-unrecorded copy). All of this is real gas and engineering, flagged as implementation work, not hand-waved. The *semantics* are fixed now, so **tokens minted at launch carry forward unchanged.**

**Binding flow (once, at acquisition):**
1. The buyer's wallet acquires the token.
2. The compliant player presents — to the **registry contract** and the **key issuer** (§4) alike: (a) a **device attestation report** proving it is a certified, integrity-checked direct player with a secure element, and (b) a fresh **wallet-signed bind authorization** naming the device, proving the wallet currently owns an unbound unit.
3. **The player writes its own bind.** The **registry contract** verifies the device-key signature and the wallet signature, checks `boundCount < balance` against the token's `balanceOf`, **increments `boundCount`**, and records a **blinded binding commitment** `C = hash(secret)` the player will later open to release — only a compliant player, authorized by the owner's wallet, can set the flag, and only that player can later release this unit. Then — *on confirmed write* — the **key issuer** verifies the same attestation and **wraps the master key to the player's secure-element public key**; a revoked player (§4) is refused at both. Flag write and key wrap are distinct acts: the fleet-written registry records the bind; the issuer only enables decryption.
4. The player unwraps the key **inside its secure element**; the film is decrypted at the decode boundary and never leaves the secure perimeter (§3) in the clear.

**Bound possession is durable.** The wrapped key is retained locally; subsequent playback contacts no network and works fully offline, indefinitely (§6). The binding is *recorded*, not *re-verified* — a bound player never phones home to play.

**Transfer and release.** A holder trades *released* units without residual. To release, the bound player deletes its wrapped key and **opens its binding commitment** to the registry (revealing the secret), which proves it is the device that bound this unit and **decrements `boundCount`** — no wallet signature, no issuer, no device identity disclosed. A protocol sale can settle through an **escrow contract** that verifies from the on-chain registry that the seller holds `≥ N` released units, **atomically** with the transfer, payment, and royalty, so a buyer transacting through it can never pay for a unit whose copy is still bound; a platform may likewise choose to list only released units, or surface a `bound` warning on the rest. The norm is *release before you list* — a coordination built on the flag, chosen by honest platforms, not imposed by the chain. **Honest boundary (the cost of freedom):** because the token always transfers freely on-chain, a **raw** transfer *outside* an escrowed sale can move a still-bound unit and break the invariant (the seller keeps a playing copy; the buyer binds another). This is detectable — the registry shows `boundCount > balance` for that wallet — forensically watermarked, and bounded to deliberate out-of-protocol behavior. It is a **permanent** physical-media-tier residual, consciously priced in as the cost of never letting the protocol block exchange — not something a later version "closes outright." (Optional fast-follow: *atomic release-at-sale* bundles release into settlement so a seller can watch until the moment of sale.)

**Device self-audit at transaction time (why "release before you list" is mostly automatic).** A compliant player also releases *proactively*. At each of its **own voluntary transactions that acquire new capability** — registering, binding a new title, releasing, reporting — it self-audits its bound titles against current on-chain ownership, and for any title whose **binding wallet** (the wallet that authorized *that* bind — not whatever wallet is currently connected, which keeps the shared-household Seed intact) no longer holds the entitling token, it **verifiably erases** the copy and **opens that title's binding commitment** to decrement `boundCount`. *Re-downloading the ciphertext of a title it already holds bound is expressly **not** such a transaction* — that is the offline-permanence path (§6), and gating it would sit an ownership check in front of playback for anyone who cleared local storage; the audit never triggers on a re-fetch of an already-bound title. So a cooperating, online device that has *sold* a title sheds that copy at its next transaction with no explicit release step, and — the honesty payoff — **an actively-used device cannot accumulate copies of titles it has sold.** This *subsumes* the reported-recovery erasure below into one mechanism: a device erases a title either because its binding wallet sold it (self-audit) or because a lost-device report released it (recovery); both are checked as a precondition of the device's next transaction. **The bright line that keeps this from becoming DRM:** the self-audit runs **only at the device's own voluntary transactions and only on confirmed on-chain non-ownership** — **never during playback, never by autonomously watching the chain.** Playback stays a pure local operation with no ownership check, so offline permanence (§6) and the no-presence-beacon guarantee below hold undisturbed; an erroneous erase from a transient bad read is recoverable (a true owner simply re-binds), so the audit fails safe.

**Recovery is wallet-authoritative and transaction-triggered — no heartbeat, no presence beacon.** A compliant player contacts the network *only* at owner-initiated transactions — registering to a wallet, downloading/binding a title, releasing one, or reporting a device — and **never emits an ambient "I'm alive" signal.** Playback always fails *open* (a present player plays its bound copies forever, offline), so only *transferability* depends on the network, and the **wallet is the root of ownership**: whoever proves control of the wallet may reclaim a unit's transferability. A device can never veto its owner. Two paths:
- **Clean release** (device present and cooperating): the device deletes its wrapped key and **opens its binding commitment**; `boundCount` decrements immediately, no residual. This is the normal path — release before you list, or before you sell/gift the hardware.
- **Reported lost, sold, or broken** (no clean release possible): the wallet owner submits a signed **report enumerating the units to recover** — the owner knows their own bound titles, so the count-only registry needs no device→title map. **30 days** later (a tunable parameter) the owner force-releases those `(wallet, tokenId)` units directly and they become transferable. The lost device, whenever it next transacts on the network, must **verifiably erase** any recovered titles as a **precondition of that transaction** (its next capability-acquiring transaction is refused until it does); a device that refuses is **revoked** (§4) and locked out. An *actively used* device therefore cannot retain recovered titles while staying online for new content.

**A real UX cost, named.** Because the self-audit keys on the binding wallet's *current* ownership, ordinary wallet hygiene has a price: moving a collection's tokens to a **cold wallet** makes the old binding wallet a non-owner, so the device sheds the whole library at its next capability-acquiring transaction and must re-bind each title from the new wallet — one issuer round-trip per title. This is a genuine cost of the design, stated so it is not a surprise; mitigations (re-bind batching; a wallet-controlled "migrate binding" that re-authorizes without full re-wrap) are an open item (§9).

**The one residual, stated honestly:** a device kept **permanently offline** never receives the erase precondition and can keep playing its copies — the irreducible limit that no system can close (an offline device cannot be forced to forget a key it holds). It is bounded and costly: the retained copy is forensically watermarked and personally attributable (§7); keeping it alive means the device is cut off from all new content, updates, and network participation forever; and the triggering report is a wallet-signed, logged, fraud-flavored act. This is the physical-media-tier residual we price in, not a redistribution vector. The precise report rate-limiting and dispute governance are an open spec item (§9).

### 2.1 Binding and the rights tiers — two regimes, one token {-}

Everything above describes the **personal-viewing regime**: bind one copy against `balanceOf ≥ 1`, playback fails open forever, the registry informs but never gates. That regime is correct *because* the residual it leaves is personal — a single retained copy, watermarked and attributable, physical-media-tier.

**Exhibition is a deliberately different regime, and the same fails-open model must not be extended to it.** Consider the loophole if it were: a venue binds a tier-*T* copy holding `threshold_T` units (e.g., 60,000 for theatrical), raw-transfers those tokens the next day, keeps the player offline, and keeps screening — retaining a *commercial exhibition* capability worth a whole rights package, not a personal copy. The personal residual analysis prices in the one-token case; it does not survive at `threshold_T`. So exhibition requires a **distinct, more-stringent unlock than personal fails-open** — one whose *mechanism is genuinely TBD and will be co-developed with the DCI community*, because DCP-grade exhibition content is far more sensitive than home content and the interface must meet the industry's established requirements (likely engaging DCI/KDM frameworks rather than reinventing them). What is *firm* is the **tiered unlock model** and the **two-regime split**: personal playback fails open (sovereign); exhibition playback is **licensed, not sovereign**. This is defensible precisely because the sovereignty thesis was always about **personal ownership**, never about commercial cinemas: a venue is a **licensee**, and the exhibition regime should meet cinema licensing where it already lives. So the personal tier's "never during playback" bright line scopes the *personal* regime; whatever exhibition enforcement emerges with DCI is a separate one, not a contradiction.

**One token, two unlocks — and that is the point.** The *same fungible token* serves both regimes; only the unlock behind each differs (personal: bind + fails-open; exhibition: a more-stringent, DCI-developed enforcement). This is what lets liquidity flow between private collectors and commercial exhibitors — a collector's tokens can aggregate into a venue's exhibition stake, giving a sleeper film its theatrical run *after* it has already circulated digitally, and flow back out again. Maximum liquidity, with the security model matched to the stakes of each use.

*Status: the exhibition regime is **forthcoming**, not built — the launch focus is home viewing. What is decided (2026-07-05) is the tiered unlock model and the two-regime split above. The exhibition **mechanism** — how a screening's entitlement is enforced, how it interfaces with DCI/KDM, how DCP-grade assets are protected, and the staking/tier accounting — is **TBD, to be worked directly with the DCI community** (§9). Whitepaper §11 states the intent, not a settled protocol.*

## 3. Device compliance requirements (the open conformance spec)

A device is **compliant** if it provides the following *capabilities* — stated as capabilities, not a specific chip, so a small-form-factor player, an SBC with a secure element, or future hardware can all conform:

- **Secure key custody.** A secure element or TEE holding a **non-exportable** device key, able to unwrap content keys **internally** and decrypt **without exposing the key or plaintext frames outside the protected perimeter**.
- **Verified boot.** Secure / measured boot, so an attestation of device state is meaningful.
- **Remote attestation.** The device can produce a verifiable report of its identity and firmware integrity that the issuer (§4) checks against the certified-device roots.
- **Output protection** appropriate to the title's tier (e.g., HDCP on HDMI), so plaintext frames are not trivially re-captured downstream.

**Reference hardware candidates** (from `PROTOCOL_LAYERS.md` Layer 0): NXP SE050, Microchip ATECC608B, ARM TrustZone, TPM 2.0. The *Origin* reference design targets ARM TrustZone plus a discrete secure element.

This section, plus a machine-checkable **conformance checklist**, is the public resource any manufacturer builds to.

## 4. Certification & issuance authority (neutral, open, federating)

At bootstrap, the **Foundation (LMF) operates two neutral functions**:
- **Device-model certification** — establishing that a device *model* meets §3, anchoring an attestation root the issuer trusts.
- **Key issuance** — the service in §2 step 3 that wraps master keys to attested, currently-entitled devices.

**Anti-gatekeeping commitments (load-bearing):**
- Certification **criteria are public** (§3); submission is **open and non-discriminatory**; every device model is certified through the same process, reference implementations included — no implementer is privileged.
- The issuer wraps to *any* compliant device presenting valid attestation and current ownership; it privileges no manufacturer.
- **Revocation** is limited to genuine compromise, recorded in an on-chain revocation registry (`PROTOCOL_LAYERS.md` §4.3, Layer 3), and transparent.

**Self-serve conformance — with an honest split between what is automated and what is reviewed.** Two things are being verified, and only one is automatable. **Identity and integrity** — that a device is a genuine recognized secure element running an *approved* firmware image — is proven by hardware attestation, which a device cannot fake: an implementer submits at the Foundation site, runs an on-device **conformance suite** whose transcript the secure element signs, and the backend verifies that attestation against the recognized roots and checks the vectors, issuing the credential automatically. A rooted, debug, or counterfeit device fails on its own; rigor there lives in the cryptography, not a gatekeeper's judgment, and a self-built rig qualifies by using a recognized root, integrated correctly. (A test-harness agent may run and self-delete; trust rests on the attested transcript, not the agent's word.)

**What attestation does *not* prove is that the device's *design* is sound.** A genuine device running honestly-measured but maliciously-*designed* firmware — one that tees decoded frames to storage, say — attests truthfully and passes the suite. That is precisely why the regimes this tier is compared to (DCI projector certification, Widevine robustness rules, Common Criteria) rest on **design review**, not attestation alone. So the model has two layers: identity/integrity conformance is automated and permissionless; **robustness certification of a firmware *design*** — deciding which firmware manifests are approved — is a **published review process** (criteria, cost, cadence, appeal), applied once per design, not per device. Reference designs ship pre-approved; a novel third-party design goes through that review, then every device built to it self-certifies automatically. This keeps the throughput promise honest about the one place human review is unavoidable — approving a *design* — rather than claiming attestation makes it disappear. The set of recognized hardware roots is, likewise, a published, criteria-based list open to new entrants.

The Foundation holding these functions at bootstrap is the neutrality choice — the access layer is stewarded by the standards body, not by any single operator — and it is explicitly a **temporary centralization**, dissolved by §5.

## 5. Decentralization roadmap (issuer → threshold)

The one genuinely centralized element at launch is a **single issuer**. This is acceptable at bootstrap because **durable possession** (§2, §6) means an issuer outage never costs a holder a film they already own. But it is not the destination. The path (matching the network-reserve staging):

1. **Bootstrap** — a single Foundation-operated issuer.
2. **Federated** — issuance is threshold-distributed across a small set of **perennial anchor nodes**; no single node can issue or withhold.
3. **Native threshold** — issuance distributes across the **Seed fleet itself** once it has the numbers: an LMAP-native threshold key network. This is where threshold sharding lands — as *hardening*, not a launch blocker — and it **restores fully ownership-cryptographic access control**, with no trusted issuer at all.

**Deploy the perennial nodes early — decoupled from the threshold crypto.** Standing up a handful of always-on anchor nodes is only marginally harder than running one, and worth doing from the start for **availability and failure-independence**: nodes in genuinely uncorrelated environments — a home connection, a solar-powered Starlink node in a remote location — do not share a datacenter's single grid, ISP, or region, so no common outage takes them all down. Two honesties keep this precise. First, *node deployment* and *trust minimization* are separable milestones: early anchor nodes run as **replicated issuers** (redundancy for uptime) before the threshold cryptography that makes them **compromise-tolerant** (no single node can issue) is ready — deploy the diverse hardware early, upgrade the trust model underneath as the crypto matures. Second, a home or remote node is physically less defensible than a datacenter, so *as pure replicas* more nodes widen the attack surface (any one compromised can issue); their security payoff is fullest once threshold makes a compromised minority harmless. So: deploy early for resilience, guard the key material until threshold lands, and let the environmental diversity become a security asset exactly when the cryptography can exploit it.

Threshold cryptography is the endgame that removes the last trusted party, reached when the fleet can support it — not a prerequisite for launch.

## 6. Permanence & durability

Consistent with whitepaper §8: a **bound** copy plays **forever, offline**, regardless of issuer, foundation, or operator fate — playback never depends on liveness. Only *re-binding* (recovering a dead player, or moving to a new one) requires the issuer to be live and, for a lost player, the recovery window (§2); the on-chain ownership record is the durable source of truth, so a holder whose player dies re-binds a new compliant player from their wallet's holdings. *The blockchain remembers; the hardware is replaceable.* (User-encrypted key-backup export — a means of issuer-independent recovery — is an **open-tier** permanence mechanism only. It is deliberately *not* offered in the compliant tier, where restoring playback from a backup would defeat one-active-copy binding.)

## 7. Threat model & honesty boundary

**Protects:** keys released only to *current* owners; keys never in plaintext at rest; decryption confined to the secure perimeter; and **per-device wrapping eliminates catalog-scale compromise** — there is no master secret whose theft unlocks the catalog, and compromising one device yields only *that* device's local content, a constant per-compromise bound (`PROTOCOL_LAYERS.md` §4.5). Stated precisely, so it is not overclaimed: the *per-compromise yield* is constant and attributable; the probability that *at least one* of a title's holders' devices is ever compromised still grows with holder count. That residual is bounded by **watermark attribution and revocation, not by cryptography** — the honest scope of "per-N security."

**Does not (stated honestly, never overclaimed):**
- Eliminate the **analog hole** (camera-at-screen). Watermarking mitigates; robustness is an arms race.
- Provide **bit-perfect copy protection** — no system does.
- **Cryptographically** guarantee one active copy against a *compromised* player. Scarcity holds because a compliant secure element honestly deletes on release; two bounded residues remain — a compromised player could report `released` while secretly retaining its copy, and the recovery path (§2) can be abused (old player kept permanently offline → recover → re-bind) for at most ~one extra copy per month — *a figure that presumes the report rate-limiting of §9, which is an open spec item; until that mechanism is set, treat the rate bound as provisional.* Both residues are watermarked and personally attributable — physical-media-tier personal leakage, not a redistribution vector — though "attributable" is itself conditional on an integrated, measured watermark scheme (§4.4 / whitepaper §7.5); and neither affects a *buyer's* guarantee of a working token (§2).

**On hard (cryptographic) revocation — a deliberate boundary, not a gap.** Cryptographically revoking a key *already delivered* to a device is not possible without either re-keying the content and requiring a fresh online fetch to keep playing, or a device that continuously re-checks a license — i.e., surveillance and non-durable possession. That is precisely how conventional DRM achieves revocation: by never granting durable ownership in the first place. Matching it would mean surrendering the sovereignty that is the protocol's reason to exist. The design therefore pushes cryptographic enforcement as far as it goes *without* becoming DRM — the single active copy is registry-gated (the contract refuses to bind beyond `boundCount ≤ balance`, and only the device that bound a unit can open its commitment to release it), and only the honest-delete-on-release step is compliance-dependent and irreducible (a secret, once given, cannot be un-given). The protocol does not compete with DRM on revocation; it offers the thing DRM structurally cannot — ownership — and is honest that the two are mutually exclusive.

No claim of "uncopyable" or "DRM-grade." Access control (ownership + binding) and endpoint protection (hardware) are never conflated.

## 9. Open questions

- **Bootstrap issuer trust** — a single issuer is a temporary central point (§5); acceptable given durable possession, but state it publicly and commit to the federation timeline.
- **Threshold scheme** — deferred design (DKG, resharing under home-device churn, liveness) for §5 step 3.
- **Certification process** — who audits, at what cost and cadence, to keep it genuinely open and non-discriminatory.
- **Public-domain legacy construction** — sunset plan for the deterministic-key demonstration path.
- **Atomic release-at-sale** (§2) — worth building for v1, or a fast-follow after release-before-listing?
- **Hard on-chain scarcity enforcement** (§2) — once the registry's writer is trustless, is a per-title, publisher-*opt-in* transfer rule ever worth offering, given the standing commitment that the protocol never blocks free exchange by default? Default answer: no protocol-wide veto, ever; opt-in-per-title is the only form to even consider.
- **Report rate-limiting and dispute governance** (§2) — bound the report-and-release path against abuse; define any contested-report adjudication.
- **Binding × rights-tiers accounting** (§2.1) — the exact `boundCount` semantics when exhibition tiers and stacking overlap.
- **Exhibition regime & DCI interface** (§2.1) — the tiered unlock model and the two-regime split are decided; the exhibition *enforcement mechanism* is TBD and must be **co-developed with the DCI community**. DCP-grade content is far more sensitive than home content; the likely path engages existing DCI/KDM frameworks rather than reinventing them. Open: how a screening's entitlement is verified and enforced at play time, how staked tokens map to a screening license, DCP asset protection, and revocation/window semantics for venues.
- **Robustness certification vs. identity attestation** (§4) — the firmware-manifest approval process for non-reference vendors (open review vs. automatic); scope the "cannot fake" claim to what attestation proves.
- **Watermarking locus** — open-tier insertion point without breaking content-addressing; a media-path (not secure-element) locus for the compliant tier; a scheme with measured robustness.
- **Oblivious issuance** — a future privacy enhancement in which the issuer does not learn the title it wraps.
- *Resolved 2026-07-05:* tier naming → "compliant"; **resale scarcity via the binding model** (§2, `boundCount` per (wallet,tokenId)); **binding-status privacy** → publish only the per-token/per-wallet released-unit status, never player identity or viewing data; **recovery model** → **transaction-triggered, no heartbeat or presence beacon** — clean release, or owner-signed report → 30-day release with transaction-gated verified erasure (Harrison, 2026-07-05); **device self-audit** generalizes transaction-gated erasure to *any* ownership change — a cooperating online device sheds a bound title at its next voluntary transaction when the *binding wallet* (not the connected wallet) no longer owns it, subsuming reported-recovery erasure into one mechanism; bright line: voluntary-transactions-only + confirmed non-ownership, never during playback, never autonomous polling (Harrison, 2026-07-05); the sole residual is a permanently-offline device, watermarked and cut off; **binding-state locus** → **on-chain, world-readable binding registry from launch**, a separate accounting contract **written by the attested compliant-player fleet** (only a compliant device + owner-wallet signature can set the flag; the contract enforces `boundCount ≤ balance`; **no central flag-writer**, so released status can never be withheld — the key issuer is confined to key-wrapping); recovery-release is the sole wallet-authoritative write. It **informs but never gates** — the protocol never lets the issuer or any party block free exchange, so scarcity for safe trade rests on platforms honoring the flag plus opt-in escrow; the reverting-transfer-hook endgame is **retired** in favor of preserving free exchange (Harrison, 2026-07-05).

## 10. Reference implementation & published resources

An **open-hardware reference design** (*Origin*) and an **open-source reference daemon** exist as the first compliant implementation. Their specifications and source are published so a manufacturer can build a compliant player, and an operator can run a storefront, without seeking anyone's permission. The conformance checklist (§3), the attestation and issuance protocol (§4), and the reference design are what the Foundation publishes for implementers. That publication is the point: the standard is the asset, and it belongs to everyone who builds on it.
